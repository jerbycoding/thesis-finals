

Recommended Tutorial Design
For novices, an integrated, story-driven tutorial works best. Instead of a dry, separate “certification” manual, we recommend a “First Day on the Job” approach (Option B) supplemented with contextual hints. Embedding the lesson into a narrative (e.g. a supportive mentor/NPC guiding the player) keeps learners engaged
. This lets us teach mechanics in context (using analogies and plain English) rather than in isolation. At the same time, we’ll provide clear, step-by-step guidance on what to do and why (the “what-how-why” of each tool
) and sprinkle in optional tooltips or hints so players can explore without getting lost
. In short, a hybrid of narrative guidance + contextual sandbox gives beginners confidence. The story makes them feel like the SOC “hero” on day one
, and the scaffolding prevents overload. We walk them through each tool (explaining the real-world analogy and the reason for each action) while leaving room to practice. This ensures they feel smart solving problems but also learn the logic behind each step (so shortcuts really do have consequences).

How would you best merge these two ideas? Should the cleanup phase involve both new terminal commands and the Decryption Tool, or would that overwhelm a new player? What is the ideal sequence to be both narratively satisfying and mechanically instructive?



Merging Mechanics: One Step at a Time
The cleanup phase should include both the new terminal commands and the Decryption Tool, but only if they’re introduced in a carefully phased way. Game UX research strongly advises teaching one mechanic at a time in a safe, controlled context to avoid overwhelming players
. In practice, that means focus first on the investigatory commands (netstat, traceroute) as the core lesson. These commands become the primary task the player must learn to track down the kill chain. Once that is well underway (and narratively justified by the player’s mistake), the Decryption Tool can then be introduced as a climactic, guided step. This “divide and conquer” approach matches best practices: introduce new features gradually and only when needed
.
1. Teach the Terminal Commands First
Begin by having the player use the new networking commands to investigate the problem. For example, the NPC might say: “Run netstat to list active connections, then use traceroute to find where the attacker came from.” Because this is new information, provide a tutorial prompt or brief hint for each command. By using a safe, guided scenario (similar to early levels of Portal), the player learns these commands without immediate failure or time pressure
. In other words, let them practice netstat and traceroute in a non-threatening context so they can experiment and build confidence
. Only after the player successfully traces the intrusion should the next phase begin.
2. Introduce the Decryption Tool as Narrative Consequence
Once the player’s investigation reveals that the cleanup requires decryption, the Decryption Tool is revealed as a story-driven solution. The NPC might solemnly comment that “the backup files were encrypted by the kill chain – we’ll need the Decryption Tool to recover them.” This moment justifies introducing a brand-new, complex tool: the player’s mistake has raised the stakes, so it makes sense narratively that a powerful (and intimidating) tool is needed. This fits the idea of contextual tutorials: new mechanics and tools only appear when relevant
. Because the Decryption Tool is complicated, its first use should be heavily guided. For example, the mentor might say, “This is complicated, so I’ll walk you through it: match the highlighted patterns to recover the files.” This provides just enough instruction to keep players from getting stuck, without dumping a wall of text. (As game UX experts note, most players prefer to “muddle through” with minimal guidance rather than read lengthy instructions
.)
3. Ideal Sequence (Step-by-Step)
Investigate the Kill Chain. The player runs netstat and traceroute under the NPC’s direction. Simple on-screen hints explain each command’s purpose. This guided investigation is the primary lesson, so keep it focused. According to tutorial design principles, don’t stack another novel puzzle on top of this – let them learn the network tracing mechanics first
.
Reveal the Consequence. After tracing, the player discovers that the attacker encrypted critical files (the damage from the Kill Chain). This narrative pay-off (“your mistake caused this!”) naturally leads to the need for decryption.
Dramatic Reveal of the Decryption Tool. The mentor character produces the Decryption Tool for the first time. This is a cinematic moment: an imposing new interface appears, emphasizing how serious the situation is.
Guided Puzzle – First Use. The NPC quickly shows how to use the tool. For example: “See these patterns? You match them to undo the encryption. I’ll highlight what you need to do.” The initial decryption puzzle should be very easy or even semi-automatic – just enough to give the player a hands-on feel without it feeling impossible. This step is essentially a one-off micro-tutorial for the new tool, which follows the recommendation to provide explicit help when a mechanic is first introduced
.
Narrative and Mechanical Closure. Once the puzzle is solved and the files are recovered, emphasize the outcome (and perhaps a lesson about care with powerful systems). The player has now used both new skills in sequence, without ever being forced to “learn both from scratch” at once.
By phasing the mechanics this way, the cleanup phase stays clear and instructive. The player’s attention isn’t split between two unknown tasks; instead, they learn the commands in a straightforward context, then get a dramatic but guided introduction to the Decryption Tool. This approach honors the narrative (making the crisis feel real) while following tutorial best practices – gradual, contextual introduction of new mechanics
. In short, teach the commands thoroughly first, and then use the Decryption Tool as a safe, scripted finale. Sources: Game tutorial design recommends introducing features gradually and in context
, and avoiding overloading the player with multiple new systems at once
. In practice, classic examples like Portal show how teaching one new mechanic at a time in a low-stress setting keeps players from frustration
, while an NPC-driven walkthrough can safely introduce a complex tool when it’s first needed
. These principles guide the sequence above.
