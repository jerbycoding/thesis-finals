### Merging the Two Ideas

The two ideas— the foundational "Certification" steps (1-20, focusing on basic tool usage) and the "Consequence Lesson" (21-23, emphasizing the Kill Chain via shortcuts and escalations)—merge best through a narrative escalation within the "First Day on the Job" framework I recommended previously. The certification builds mechanical confidence, while the consequence lesson layers in strategic thinking about repercussions, creating a holistic "hero's journey" where the player starts as a novice, makes a relatable mistake, faces consequences, and emerges wiser. This merger avoids feeling like two separate modules by framing the consequence as a direct narrative payoff to the certification: after "passing" basics, a "real-world" test reveals why procedures matter.

### On the Cleanup Phase: Including Terminal Commands and Decryption Tool

For the cleanup in step 23 (handling the escalated malware from the shortcut), I recommend **starting with new Terminal commands only, and teasing the Decryption Tool as an optional or future escalation**, rather than requiring both immediately. Introducing both could overwhelm beginners, as it doubles the cognitive load: Terminal involves command-line logic (active defense), while the Decryption Tool adds puzzle-solving (hex-based decryption for ransomware). New players are still internalizing basics like scanning and isolating—layering a puzzle might feel like a genre shift from investigation to mini-game, disrupting flow and causing frustration if they're not ready. Instead:
- Use Terminal for core cleanup (e.g., 'trace' to identify spread, 'purge' to remove malware), reinforcing earlier lessons.
- Hint at the Decryption Tool via NPC dialogue (e.g., "If this had gone ransomware-level, we'd need the Decryptor—good thing we caught it early!"), planting seeds for later without mandating it here. This keeps the phase focused, instructive, and scalable—if the player excels, an optional branch could introduce Decryption lightly.
This balances accessibility: Terminal feels like an evolution of prior steps (e.g., from 'scan' to 'trace/purge'), while reserving Decryption for when players feel "pro" enough for puzzles.
